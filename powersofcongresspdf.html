Case Study
Congress In Real Life
Epstein Files

The Epstein Files Transparency Act

A real life, messy, controversial example of how a bill moves through Congress and challenges the limits of congressional power.

Today you are working with a case that is taboo, politically risky, and full of drama. Perfect for learning how a bill tries to become a law.

02

Essential Question

How does Congress use its power to force the release of highly sensitive information, and what does this teach us about the process a bill must follow to become a law in the United States?

Learning Targets

  • Describe the steps a bill takes from the House to the Senate to the President.
  • Explain why the Epstein Files Transparency Act is controversial and important.
  • Identify how Congress checks the executive branch in this case.
03

What Are The “Epstein Files”?

The Epstein files are federal records connected to Jeffrey Epstein, a financier convicted of sex crimes who later died in jail.

  • Investigation records from the FBI and DOJ.
  • Flight logs, emails, and communications.
  • Internal government memos about how his case was handled.
  • Materials related to Ghislaine Maxwell and other possible co conspirators.

The files may reveal how far Epstein’s network went and what the government did or did not do.

04

Why Do These Files Matter?

  • Victims want to know who helped or protected Epstein.
  • The public needs to trust that the justice system is not protecting the powerful.
  • Releasing the files could expose high profile names and destroy reputations.
  • This makes the case politically dangerous but also a powerful civics lesson.
When information is this sensitive, every step of the congressional process becomes a battle.
05

Cast Of Characters - Department Of Justice

The Department of Justice (DOJ) runs the federal investigation and decides what to release.

  • Headed by the Attorney General.
  • Controls the official Epstein files and how much is made public.
  • Released a “first phase” of files that were heavily redacted and mostly old news.
DOJ’s Position

DOJ says it must protect victim identities and ongoing investigations, but critics say it is stonewalling and hiding the real story.

06

Cast Of Characters - House Of Representatives

The House of Representatives is the chamber where the Epstein Files Transparency Act starts.

  • Rep. Ro Khanna introduces the bill H.R. 4405.
  • Many Democrats and some Republicans support full transparency.
  • The bill says DOJ must publish all unclassified Epstein records online.
This is where the drama starts: a bill that pokes at powerful people and a sensitive case.
07

Cast Of Characters - House Leadership

Speaker Mike Johnson and House leadership control which bills get a vote.

  • At first, leadership is not eager to bring the Epstein bill to the floor.
  • Some members accuse leadership of protecting powerful names or avoiding controversy.
  • This leads to a rare power move: the discharge petition.

Agenda control is quiet power. If leadership does nothing, a bill can quietly die.

08

Cast Of Characters - Senate

The Senate is the slower, more cautious chamber that also has to approve the bill.

  • Has its own version of the Epstein Files Transparency Act.
  • Uses the filibuster, which often requires 60 votes to move forward.
  • Can amend or delay the bill, even after the House passes it.

The Senate can cool down the drama or kill the bill quietly through procedure.

09

Cast Of Characters - The President

The President is the final gatekeeper in the normal process.

  • Can sign the bill into law.
  • Can veto the bill and send it back to Congress.

In this case, President Donald Trump and his team have signaled that they do not want a full public document dump. That makes a veto very likely if the bill reaches his desk.

10

What The Act Actually Does

The Epstein Files Transparency Act (H.R. 4405) is not just a suggestion.

  • Orders the DOJ to publish all unclassified Epstein investigation records online.
  • Requires the files to be searchable and downloadable by the public.
  • Allows redactions only to protect victim identities, child sexual abuse material, and narrow security issues.

This turns the public’s curiosity into a legal demand for transparency.

11

Resolution Versus Law

  • H.Res. 577 is a House resolution that demands release of Epstein documents.
  • Resolutions are mostly symbolic. They show what the House feels, not what agencies must do.
  • The Transparency Act is a bill. If passed and signed, it becomes a law that the DOJ must follow.
A resolution says “we want this.” A law says “you are required to do this.”
12

Why Congress Needs A Law Here

The executive branch could choose to release more, but it has not.

  • DOJ insists it has already given enough and that it must protect sensitive information.
  • Survivors and lawmakers argue that DOJ is hiding crucial details.
  • Congress responds by using its power to write a law that forces disclosure.

This is checks and balances in real time.

13

Two Types Of Power - Oversight And Lawmaking

Oversight Power
  • Hold hearings and question officials.
  • Issue subpoenas for documents.
  • Release some documents to the public through committees.
Lawmaking Power
  • Write a bill that tells an agency what it must do.
  • Pass that bill in the House and Senate.
  • Send it to the President to sign into law.

In this case, Congress is using both powers at the same time to pry open the Epstein files.

14

Timeline - Early 2025

“We gave you something. Is that not enough?”

  • DOJ releases a “first phase” of declassified Epstein documents.
  • Most are heavily redacted or already known from earlier leaks.
  • Survivors and activists call it a half truth, not real transparency.

This partial release sets the stage for Congress to step in more aggressively.

15

Timeline - Congress Subpoenas The Files

The House Oversight Committee uses its subpoena power to demand Epstein records from DOJ.

  • DOJ sends tens of thousands of pages.
  • The committee releases over 30,000 pages to the public.
  • Reporters discover that only a small percentage is actually new information.

Oversight alone is not enough. That is why lawmakers start pushing for a law.

16

Timeline - The Bill Is Introduced

Rep. Ro Khanna introduces the Epstein Files Transparency Act (H.R. 4405).

  • Shifts the fight from “please release more” to “you must release more.”
  • Backed by members who frame it as a battle for truth and justice for survivors.
  • Immediately becomes politically sensitive inside Congress.
17

Timeline - Leadership Stalls

House leadership, including the Speaker, does not rush to schedule a vote.

  • Some say the bill is unnecessary after partial releases.
  • Others quietly worry about what might be exposed.
  • Supporters accuse leadership of protecting powerful interests or playing politics.
This is where procedure becomes drama. A controversial bill can be buried without a single vote.
18

The Discharge Petition Plot Twist

When leadership will not move a bill, representatives can sign a discharge petition.

  • Needs 218 signatures, a majority of the House.
  • If it hits 218, it forces the bill out of committee and onto the floor.
  • Used rarely because it is a direct challenge to leadership’s control.

For the Epstein bill, the petition reaches the magic number, shocking leadership and triggering a promised vote.

19

Step 1 - House Debate And Vote

Once the bill is on the floor, the House debates and votes.

  • Members give speeches for and against transparency.
  • Amendments can be proposed to change parts of the bill.
  • The bill needs a simple majority of votes to pass.

If it passes, the drama moves to the next level: the Senate.

20

Step 2 - Senate Obstacles

In the Senate, the bill faces new rules and new risks.

  • Goes to a committee, which can hold hearings or sit on it.
  • Senators can use the filibuster to block a final vote.
  • To break a filibuster usually takes 60 votes to end debate.

Even if a simple majority likes the bill, it can die if 60 votes are not there to move it.

21

Step 2b - House And Senate Agreement

Both chambers must approve the same text.

  • If the Senate changes the bill, the House must agree to those changes.
  • Sometimes a conference committee works out a compromise version.
  • Both chambers vote again on the final unified bill.

Only then is the bill ready for the President’s desk.

22

Step 3 - President: Sign Or Veto

A passed bill goes to the President.

  • Sign the bill - it becomes law.
  • Veto the bill - it is rejected and sent back to Congress.

In this case, the President has a strong reason to veto a bill that orders a full public release of sensitive files.

23

Step 4 - Veto Override

Congress has one last move if the President vetoes the bill.

  • They can vote to override the veto.
  • This takes a two thirds majority in both the House and the Senate.
  • That is much higher than a simple majority and requires strong bipartisan support.

If they cannot reach two thirds, the veto stands and the bill dies.

24

Myth Busting - “Reconciliation” And Vetoes

  • Myth: After a veto, Congress just uses “reconciliation” to fix it.
  • Fact: Reconciliation is a special budget process, not a veto repair tool.
  • After a veto, Congress either gets two thirds to override or writes a new bill and starts again.

The Epstein case shows how important it is to know the difference between real procedures and political slogans.

25

Myth Busting - “Congress Can Just Post It”

  • Myth: Congress can post all the Epstein files whenever it wants.
  • Fact: Committees are still bound by rules about classification and victim privacy.
  • That is why they prefer a law that tells the DOJ exactly what must be published and how.

The law puts responsibility directly on the agency that owns the files.

26

Drama Hooks - Why This Case Hits Nerves

  • Involves sex trafficking, minors, and powerful people.
  • Raises questions about elite protection and double standards in justice.
  • Tests whether politicians will risk embarrassment for transparency.
  • Makes the basic “How a Bill Becomes a Law” chart feel very real and very tense.
27

Civics Takeaway 1 - Congress Has Multiple Jobs

  • Lawmaker: writes and passes bills.
  • Watchdog: investigates and questions the executive branch.
  • Voice of the people: responds to victims, advocates, and public pressure.

The Epstein Files Transparency Act forces Congress to do all three at once.

28

Civics Takeaway 2 - Agenda Control Is Power

  • Leadership can stall or bury a bill by not scheduling it.
  • A discharge petition lets a majority of members fight back.
  • In this case, rank and file representatives used that tool to force a vote.

This shows how internal rules can decide what the public ever gets to see.

29

Civics Takeaway 3 - Checks And Balances

  • The executive branch controls the files.
  • The legislative branch wants transparency and uses lawmaking power.
  • Both sides test how far their power goes in a high stakes case.

Checks and balances are not abstract here. They decide how much truth the public will get.

Exit Ticket - Reflect On The Case

Answer at least one of these in your notes:

  • What is one step in the process where you think the Epstein bill is most likely to fail and why?
  • Which branch of government shows the most power in this case study and how?
  • Do you think the public has a right to see all unclassified Epstein files? Explain your reasoning.
This is not just about one scandal. It is about how our system handles truth when the truth is dangerous.
