Base your answers to questions 11 and 12 on the excerpts below and on your knowledge of social studies.
“An Act to secure homesteads to actual settlers on the public domain.” Any adult head of a family, or a person twenty‑one years of age, who applied for a homestead and met the law’s conditions, could claim up to 160 acres and obtain title after residence and cultivation. As the statute states, “no certificate shall be given or patent issued therefor until the expiration of five years from the date of such entry.”
Source: U.S. Statutes at Large, 12 Stat. 392 (1862); transcript via National Archives, “Homestead Act (1862).”
“An Act to provide for the allotment of lands in severalty to Indians on the various reservations.” The law authorized individual allotments and placed them under a federal trust period before fee‑simple title. It specified that “the United States will hold the land thus allotted, for the period of twenty‑five years, in trust for the use and benefit of the Indian,” after which a patent in fee would issue; unallotted “surplus” reservation lands could be negotiated for sale by the United States.
Source: 24 Stat. 388 (1887), Indian General Allotment (Dawes) Act; text reflecting Secs. 5–6 via govinfo.gov.

Stimulus A — President Andrew Jackson, Message to Congress on Indian Removal (December 6, 1830) Source: U.S. National Archives, “President Andrew Jackson's Message to Congress 'On Indian Removal' (1830),” transcript. “It gives me pleasure to announce to Congress that the benevolent policy of the Government... in relation to the removal of the Indians beyond the white settlements is approaching to a happy consummation... The consequences of a speedy removal will be important to the United States, to individual States, and to the Indians themselves... It puts an end to all possible danger of collision between the authorities of the General and State Governments on account of the Indians. It will place a dense and civilized population in large tracts of country now occupied by a few savage hunters... By opening the whole territory between Tennessee on the north and Louisiana on the south to the settlement of the whites it will incalculably strengthen the southwestern frontier... It will relieve the whole State of Mississippi and the western part of Alabama of Indian occupancy... It will separate the Indians from immediate contact with settlements of whites; free them from the power of the States; enable them to pursue happiness in their own way and under their own rude institutions; will retard the progress of decay... and perhaps cause them... to cast off their savage habits and become an interesting, civilized, and Christian community.” Reduced excerpt of about 200 words with ellipses to indicate omissions. Full transcript: archives.gov/milestone-documents/jacksons-message-to-congress-on-indian-removal
Stimulus B — Indian Removal Act (May 28, 1830) Source: National Archives DocsTeach, enrolled act transcript. “An Act to provide for an exchange of lands with the Indians... and for their removal West of the river Mississippi. Be it enacted... That it shall and may be lawful for the President of the United States to cause so much of any territory belonging to the United States, west of the river Mississippi... to be divided into a suitable number of districts, for the reception of such tribes or nations of Indians as may choose to exchange the lands where they now reside... And be it further enacted, That it shall and may be lawful for the President to exchange any or all of such districts with any tribe or nation of Indians now residing within the limits of any of the states or territories... for the whole or any part or portion of the territory claimed and occupied by such tribe or nation... And be it further enacted, That the President... shall cause such aid and assistance to be furnished to the emigrants as may be necessary and proper... and that compensation shall be made for their improvements... And be it further enacted, That the United States will forever secure and guarantee to them... the country so exchanged with them; Provided... that nothing in this act... shall be construed to violate any existing treaty.” Reduced excerpt of about 200 words with ellipses to indicate omissions.
